# LinkedIn Drafts — Hotel CMBS Distress (Trepp 2026)

## Draft 1 — Luke Thompson

The headline number from Trepp's March 2026 release: lodging CMBS delinquency printed 7.31 percent, up 137 basis points in a single month after a 105-bps drop in January.

That volatility is itself the story. A single SASB hotel deal moves the lodging series 50 to 100 bps. The trend matters more than any monthly print.

The number underneath the headline is the more useful one: lodging special servicing sat at 9.37 percent in January, down from a 10.61 percent peak in October — the highest level since March 2022.

When special servicing compresses ahead of delinquency, modifications are clearing the pipeline before loans go non-performing. That is what we have watched through the first four months of 2026.

Contrarian read: the distressed hotel acquisition opportunity that the headlines advertise — Sun Belt select-service trading at fire-sale cap rates — does not exist in the data. The transfers are concentrated in mid-tier full-service in tertiary CBDs. The Starwood $577M and $265M portfolio modifications, the Park Hotels SF walk-away — those are the cycle's defining names, and none of them are Hampton Inns in Texas.

If you have a 2026 or 2027 CMBS maturity, run a refinance pro forma and a sale BOV in parallel. The proceeds gap on a 2018-vintage loan is 12 to 20 percent at current pricing.

## Draft 2 — Nate Solomon

Three numbers from the Trepp public data that every hotel owner should be tracking right now.

One: 30 percent of CMBS loans backed by hotels mature in 2026 — a substantially higher share than any other property type (Trepp).

Two: lodging is 20.5 percent of the $76.6 billion 2026 hard-maturity book and carries the highest weighted-average debt yield at 12.93 percent (Trepp). Translation: the maturing book has the lowest in-place loan-to-value cushion of any property type.

Three: a 2018-vintage loan refinances at 150 to 250 bps higher coupon, against an LTV that is 5 to 10 points tighter, at a cap rate that is 50 to 100 bps wider. The proceeds gap runs 12 to 20 percent of prior balance.

What this means for owners: the math has changed enough that for a meaningful share of the maturing book, selling at current cap rates produces a better outcome than writing a fresh equity check.

What it means for buyers: the distress is real, but it is concentrated in full-service urban CBD names and in portfolio recapitalizations. The stabilized Sun Belt secondary select-service that everyone wants to buy at distressed pricing is clearing through normal sale and refinance — not through workout.
