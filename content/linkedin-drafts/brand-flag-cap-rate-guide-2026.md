## Draft 1 — Luke Thompson

Hampton, Holiday Inn Express, and Marriott select-service flags are clearing the market at meaningfully different cap rates in 2026, and the gap is not random.

Stabilized PIP-current select-service is trading in a 7.25 to 8.75 percent cap range right now, per HVS US Market Pulse and CBRE's H2 2025 Cap Rate Survey. Sun Belt secondary is roughly 75 basis points wider than primary urban. Tertiary is another 50 to 100 basis points wider than secondary.

Inside the same Sun Belt secondary market, Hampton typically trades 25 to 50 bps tighter than Holiday Inn Express. The driver is loyalty mix. Hilton Honors contributes 60 to 70 percent of room nights at a stabilized Hampton; IHG One Rewards runs 50 to 60 percent at HIE. Effective franchise load lines up: Hampton at ~10 percent, HIE at 9.5 to 10 percent, Marriott select between 8 and 11 percent depending on flag. Every 100 basis points of franchise load is real per-key value.

Contrarian point: most owners I talk to overestimate how much the flag itself moves their cap rate, and underestimate how much within-flag positioning does. STR index against the local comp set moves valuation more than the brand letter on the front of the building.

Which lever do you weight more in your underwriting: brand selection or within-flag positioning?

## Draft 2 — Nate Solomon

Sellers ask me the same question every week: what cap rate will my Hampton trade at in 2026? Here is the working answer.

Stabilized, PIP-current, in a Sun Belt secondary market: 7.50 to 8.50 percent (HVS US Market Pulse, April 2026; CBRE H2 2025 Cap Rate Survey). Same property in a primary urban Sun Belt metro: 7.25 to 8.00 percent. In a tertiary market: 8.25 to 9.25 percent. Holiday Inn Express trades 25 to 50 bps wider than Hampton in the same market; Fairfield Inn & Suites is comparable to HIE; Courtyard sits closer to Hampton.

The variable that moves the answer most is not the flag. It is PIP status. A PIP-overhang property (renovation due in 18 months or less) clears 50 to 125 basis points wider than a PIP-current peer. Practical effect on a $15M asset: the PIP overhang costs the seller roughly $2 to $3.5M of headline value. Most owners I work with do not run this math early enough.

Contrarian take: the highest-return move for a lot of select-service owners right now is not selling, it is completing the PIP, holding for two years, and selling into a tighter market with a clean franchise-license extension on file.

What is your PIP cycle telling you about your sell window?
