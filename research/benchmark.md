# Competitive Benchmark — Hospitality CRE Brokerage Sites

Audited against three best-in-class hospitality / commercial real estate brokerage sites to identify what mghotelteam.com lacks and where the entire category is still soft enough that we can leapfrog.

Sites audited:
- Hodges Ward Elliott (HWE) — https://www.hodgeswardelliott.com/
- Hunter Hotel Advisors — https://hunteradvisors.co/ (formerly hunterhotels.net, redirects)
- JLL Hotels & Hospitality — https://www.jll.com/en-us/property-types/hotels-hospitality

---

## 1. Hodges Ward Elliott (HWE)

The institutional standard for boutique hospitality advisory. Deep, narrative-driven, but visually undercooked.

### What works
- **Transactions hub with multi-axis filtering** (https://www.hodgeswardelliott.com/transactions/). Four dropdowns: Transaction Type (Investment Sale, Debt Placement, Equity, Construction Financing), Asset Type (Hotels split into Full Service / Resort / Select Service, plus Office/Multifamily/Retail/Medical/Dev Site), Location, and Client Profile (PE / REIT / Developer / Family Office / Corporate). The Client Profile filter is unusual and smart — it tells a sponsor "we've done deals for people like you."
- **Team page filters by Office and Team Type** (Hotel vs Capital Markets) across 22 professionals in 9 offices including London (https://www.hodgeswardelliott.com/team/). This is the right pattern for a multi-office shop.
- **Business Lines page uses named case studies inline** (https://www.hodgeswardelliott.com/business-lines/) — Liberty Hotel, Margaritaville Beach Resort, Harvest Inn appear as proof points within the services narrative, not as a separate "case studies" graveyard.
- **Hard credibility numbers stated plainly**: "over 1,800 hotel transactions since 1990," "$100B+ closed," "$50B since 2015." No dashboards, just specific and audited-feeling.

### What's polished
- Clean, restrained typography with generous white space.
- SVG-based logo and chrome — modern build hygiene.
- Narrative hierarchy: bold headline / value prop / supporting paragraph / inline case study mention / CTA. Reads like a deck.

### Distinctive
- The "Client Profile" filter on Transactions is unique — none of the other sites segment closed deals by sponsor type.
- They publicly mourn deceased leadership on the news index (Mark Elliott obituary as featured news). Humanizing in a category that usually isn't.

### What's still dated or weak
- **No property images on listing cards.** The transaction cards show a generic "H" icon with name, location, asset type, room count. In 2026 this is shocking — the most photogenic asset class in CRE shown without photos.
- **Team detail pages appear broken or thin** — common URL patterns return 404 (e.g., /team/will-rogers/, /team/mark-elliott/), and the index only exposes name + title + headshot placeholder. No deal track record, no transaction volume, no specialty surfaced from the index.
- **No market intelligence section at all.** Zero research, zero quarterly reports, zero charts. For a firm that closes $50B+ in deals, this is the biggest miss.

## 2. Hunter Hotel Advisors

Tightest positioning of the three. Best gated funnel. Weakest data transparency.

### What works
- **Hero: "Hotels are all we do."** No clearer positioning statement exists in the category. Followed by "the top hotel-only brokerage firm in the U.S." Hunter wins at one-line positioning.
- **Listings page with three filters that actually match how buyers think** (https://hunteradvisors.co/listings/): Broker, Segment (Boutique / Economy / Extended Stay / Full Service / Luxury / Select Service — exactly the chain-scale taxonomy buyers use), Region (Midwest / Northeast / South / West).
- **"Upcoming dates" section** distinguishes deals with scheduled tours / offer deadlines from evergreen current listings. That's a calendar-driven cue buyers and lenders read.
- **Team bios are the deepest of any site audited.** Each broker entry on https://hunteradvisors.co/about/ includes: years of experience, transaction volume ("more than $13 billion"), sector expertise (select-service / full-service / portfolios / distressed), notable institutional clients (Blackstone, Starwood, Marriott named publicly), prior employer history, named transactions, education and certifications (e.g., CHB designation), board memberships, and geographic specialization.
- **Teague Talks** — a YouTube interview series with industry executives. Continuous content engine, not just press releases.
- **Hunter Conference** — they run the 38th Annual Hunter Conference. Brokerage + conference is a defensible flywheel none of the others have.

### What's polished
- Coral/teal palette and abstract brand graphics give the site an identity instead of generic-CRE-blue.
- Property card photography is actually present (unlike HWE).
- Clean sans-serif hierarchy, readable on mobile.
- Listings push to a Revere CRE deal room, which is a polished third-party deal-marketing platform — the seam is well-handled.

### Distinctive
- **Tightest gate in the category**: a modal blocks "full details, location insights, and investment highlights" until you submit name / company / email / phone. This trades soft browse for hard lead capture, and they've decided lead capture wins.
- **Conference + brokerage flywheel** is a moat the others don't have.

### What's still dated or weak
- **No price, no key count, no cap rate, no ADR/RevPAR shown on listing cards.** Even the gated state would benefit from teaser stats. They've optimized for form fills at the cost of qualifying clicks.
- **No market intelligence / research PDFs.** Same blind spot as HWE — for a firm with 50 years and $13B+ tracked, a quarterly hotel investment report writes itself.
- **No filtering on news/press**, just chronological feed.

## 3. JLL Hotels & Hospitality

Big-firm scale, big-firm caution. Looks corporate, not specialist.

### What works
- **Three quantified credibility stats above the fold**: 400+ hospitality professionals globally, $19B production volume in 2024, 290 deals closed globally in 2024. Annualized and current — a moving target the others don't keep updated.
- **Two-button hero CTA: "View properties for sale" / "View properties for lease."** Cleanest jobs-to-be-done split of the three sites.
- **Insight-led structure** with named research products: "Global Hotel Investment Outlook 2026" and "Global Real Estate Perspective" (Feb 2025). Both link to dedicated insight pages with predictable cadence implied.
- **People discovery URL is parameterized and filterable**: /en-us/people.html?location=United+States&propertyTypes=Hotels+and+Hospitality. Buyers can filter the entire firm by sector AND geography. None of the boutiques can match this.
- **Tourism & destination advisory** sits next to brokerage — broader strategic-advisory footprint than pure transaction shops.

### What's polished
- Responsive image pipeline (webp, DPR-aware, lazy loading, Cloudinary/Scene7 CDN). Build hygiene is enterprise-grade.
- Clean corporate typography, mid-market SaaS aesthetic.
- Named senior leaders surface high (Kevin Davis, Daniel Peek) with photos and direct email links — the firm doesn't hide behind "contact us" forms at the top.

### Distinctive
- **Tourism & destination advisory subsection** — not just deal brokerage, but advisory to municipalities and tourism boards. Strategic surface area no boutique has.
- **Property listings live on a separate domain** (property.jll.com) — full marketplace, not a website appendage. They've split inventory from marketing.

### What's still dated or weak
- **Generic stock cityscape hero** with no specific property in it. For a hotel investment leader, the hero should be a recognizable trophy asset, not a blue-hour skyline anyone could buy on Shutterstock.
- **No client logos, testimonials, case studies, or third-party rankings on the landing page.** All trust signals are self-reported. On a $19B production volume claim, social proof is suspiciously absent.

## Top 1% patterns to steal

Patterns observed on 2+ sites that mghotelteam.com currently lacks:

1. **Multi-axis filterable transactions/closed-deal database.** Both HWE and Hunter expose filtered closed-deal pages. The right axes for hospitality are: Year (range), Chain Scale / Segment (Luxury, Full Service, Select Service, Extended Stay, Economy, Boutique), Region, Brand, Keys (range), Deal Size (range or "Upon Request"), Transaction Type (Sale / Debt / Equity), and — copying HWE's smart move — Sponsor Profile (PE / REIT / Developer / Family Office). Build it as a single data table that powers both Closed Deals and Active Listings views.
2. **Team page filterable by Office AND Specialty.** HWE filters by Office + Team Type. JLL filters their entire firm by Property Type + Location via URL parameters. Mghotelteam should let buyers find "the Florida select-service guy" or "the West Coast luxury resort person" in two clicks.
3. **Deep individual broker pages with track-record depth.** Hunter is the gold standard here. Each broker page should expose: years of experience, named transaction volume (e.g., "$2.4B in the past 12 months"), sector specialty (e.g., select-service portfolios, distressed assets), institutional clients named publicly when permissible, education + designations (CHB, CCIM, MAI), notable named deals with year and chain scale, board memberships, languages spoken (Hunter does't actually do languages — opportunity to leapfrog), direct phone, direct email, LinkedIn, recent news/closings filtered to that broker.
4. **Quantified credibility in the hero, kept current.** JLL's "$19B / 290 deals / 400+ professionals in 2024" is the move. Update it annually. Specific year matters.
5. **Named case studies woven into the services narrative**, not exiled to a separate page. HWE drops "Liberty Hotel / Margaritaville Beach Resort / Harvest Inn" inline in the Business Lines page. The pattern: don't make people click through to find proof.
6. **Brand-segmented content engine beyond press releases.** Hunter's Teague Talks (YouTube interview series) and the Hunter Conference are flywheels. The minimum bar: a regular hotel investment outlook PDF with downloadable executive summary and chart-rich web version.
7. **"Upcoming dates" / deal-calendar surface.** Hunter does this on listings. Buyers in this category are deadline-driven (call-for-offers dates, tour windows). Surfacing the calendar publicly is high-signal.
8. **Gated lead capture for full deal materials, but with teaser data exposed.** Hunter gates everything; they leave clicks on the table because there's nothing to qualify against. The right pattern: show keys, market, segment, year built, asking price (or "Upon Request"), photo, broker, and a one-line teaser publicly — gate the OM, financials, and detailed market study behind a CA / form.

## Patterns to leapfrog (none of these CRE sites do this well)

Tech-product polish the category hasn't caught up to:

1. **Listing detail page anatomy that actually respects the asset class.** None of the audited sites do justice to how photogenic hotels are. Steal from Sotheby's International Realty / Kuper / Compass: full-bleed photo hero, lightbox gallery with 30+ shots, embedded fly-through video, site plan + floor plate, brand standards spec sheet. Above the fold on the right rail: keys / brand / chain scale / year built / year renovated / ADR (or "Upon Request") / RevPAR / occupancy / asking price (or "Upon Request") / encumbrance status / management contract status / franchise term remaining / call-for-offers date.
2. **Sticky broker contact rail on listing pages.** Photo + name + title + direct phone + direct email + "Request OM" CTA. Pinned to the right on desktop, becomes a sticky bottom bar on mobile. Not one CRE site we audited had this — they all bury the broker.
3. **Better motion than CRE convention.** Subtle scroll-triggered reveals on stat counters, gentle parallax on hero photography, smooth section transitions. Linear-tier motion, not "fade-in-on-scroll Wix template." HWE and Hunter both feel static; JLL has zero motion.
4. **Better typography.** All three sites use generic corporate sans-serifs. A serif display face for headlines (think Söhne / GT Sectra pairing) with a clean grotesk for body would instantly signal premium specialist over corporate generalist. Walden Retreats already proved this works for the team — same playbook here.
5. **Photography presentation.** Full-bleed editorial heroes featuring actual closed-deal trophies, not stock skylines. Lightbox galleries with cinematic transitions. Black-letterboxed photo treatment for the listings index. Hire one shoot day per major deal, reuse across BOV / website / social / press.
6. **Clean Apple-Support-style forms.** "Request OM" should be 4 fields max with smart defaults: Name (auto-formatted), Email (validated inline), Firm (autocomplete from Crunchbase-style list), Capacity (Principal / Advisor / Lender / Other). One-tap on mobile, no captcha unless flagged. Hunter's modal works but feels 2016.
7. **Better mobile listing browse.** A hotel-buyer in an airport lounge wants to swipe through 20 listings card-by-card with photos, then tap the broker's name to call directly. None of the audited sites design for that motion.
8. **Live deal volume ticker or "in market now" counter** that pulls from the deal database. JLL has 2024 stats baked in but they're stale within 12 months. A live counter ("$2.1B in market across 14 active assignments") repositions the firm as currently active, not historically active.
9. **Market intelligence section as a publication, not a footnote.** Quarterly hotel investment outlook with: interactive cap-rate chart by chain scale, ADR/RevPAR trend by market, transaction volume heat map, expert commentary (video + transcript), downloadable PDF executive summary. None of the three sites have this — wide open category leadership opportunity.
10. **Press section with featured story treatment.** All three sites do chronological-only press feeds. Even one curated "Featured Coverage" tile + filter-by-publication would feel modern.

## Specific recommendations for the Matthews build

Concrete decisions our build should make based on this research:

1. **Listing detail pages anchor on a specific data block above the fold**: Keys, Brand, Chain Scale, Year Built, Year Renovated, ADR (or "Upon Request"), RevPAR (or "Upon Request"), Occupancy, Asking Price (or "Upon Request"), Encumbrance Status, Management/Franchise Status, and a "Request OM" CTA. None of the three audited sites do this above the fold — Hunter doesn't show any of it publicly, HWE shows only keys, JLL doesn't surface inventory at all on the property-type page. This is uncontested space.
2. **Sticky broker contact rail on every listing detail page**, with photo / name / direct phone / direct email / "Request OM" button. Zero of the audited sites do this; all three bury the broker.
3. **Closed Deals page is one filterable database with at minimum**: Year (range), Segment, Region, Brand, Keys (range), Deal Size (range or "Confidential"), Transaction Type, Sponsor Profile. Steal HWE's Sponsor Profile axis specifically — it's the smartest filter in the category and nobody else has it.
4. **Team detail pages must include named transaction track record.** Each broker gets: career transaction volume, last-12-months volume, top 10 named deals (year, asset, chain scale, location, role), institutional clients named where permissible, designations and education, languages spoken, direct phone/email, LinkedIn, and a feed of news/closings filtered to that broker. Hunter is the bar; we should clear it.
5. **Hero stats kept current with year stamp** ("$X.XB closed in 2025 / N transactions / N markets"). Steal JLL's pattern, fix their staleness problem by making the numbers a CMS field with a "last updated" date visible.
6. **Quarterly Hotel Investment Outlook as a publication.** Web-native with embedded charts (cap rates by chain scale, ADR/RevPAR by market, transaction volume), plus downloadable PDF. None of HWE, Hunter, or JLL-on-this-page have this. Owning quarterly cadence here is brand-defining.
7. **Photography is non-negotiable on every listing card.** HWE's icon-only transaction cards are the cautionary tale. We never ship a card without a hero photo, even if it's a placeholder for upcoming launches ("Renderings coming Q2").
8. **Gate the OM, not the listing.** Show keys / brand / segment / market / asking-or-request / photo / broker publicly. Form-gate the full OM, financials, and market study. Hunter over-gates and loses qualifying clicks; the right line is "browse for free, OM costs an email."
